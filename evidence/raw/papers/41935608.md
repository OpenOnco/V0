# The role of ctDNA in precision adjuvant therapy for colorectal cancer: a scoping review.

## Abstract

**BACKGROUND**: Recurrence after curative-intent treatment for colorectal cancer (CRC) is largely driven by minimal residual disease (MRD). Circulating tumor DNA (ctDNA) offers a noninvasive approach to detect MRD and tailor adjuvant therapy and surveillance.

**METHODS**: This scoping review synthesizes prospective cohorts, interventional/randomized trials, and real-world registries on ctDNA-based MRD testing in resected CRC, comparing tumor-informed versus plasma-only (mutation- and/or methylation-based) assays.

**RESULTS**: Postoperative ctDNA positivity strongly predicts recurrence and often precedes radiologic relapse by ~3-10 months. Tumor-informed assays provide high specificity and analytical sensitivity, whereas plasma-only assays enable tissue-free, faster testing but often require serial sampling. In stage II colon cancer, ctDNA-guided management reduced chemotherapy use without compromising recurrence-free survival. ctDNA dynamics add prognostic resolution: clearance after adjuvant therapy is associated with excellent outcomes, whereas persistent positivity signals very high relapse risk. Escalation approaches tested to date (e.g., intensified chemotherapy or trifluridine/tipiracil) have not shown definitive disease-free survival gains in ctDNA-positive patients. Implementation data indicate ctDNA can influence treatment and surveillance decisions, but barriers include assay variability, optimal timing, counseling, and reimbursement.

**CONCLUSION**: ctDNA-based MRD testing is a robust prognostic tool and a practical framework for biology-guided postoperative CRC care; ongoing phase III trials should establish standardized algorithms and effective MRD-directed therapies.
